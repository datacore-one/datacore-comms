# StoryBrand Guide

Strategic messaging framework based on Donald Miller's 7-part storytelling structure.

## Core Principle

**The customer is the HERO. Your brand is the GUIDE.**

Most brands make the mistake of positioning themselves as the hero. But customers don't want another hero - they want a guide who can help them win.

Think: Frodo is the hero, Gandalf is the guide. Luke is the hero, Yoda is the guide.

---

## The SB7 Formula

Every story follows this pattern:

```
A CHARACTER (1) who wants something
encounters a PROBLEM (2) before they can get it.
At the peak of their despair, a GUIDE (3) steps into their life,
gives them a PLAN (4),
and CALLS THEM TO ACTION (5).
That action helps them avoid FAILURE (6)
and ends in SUCCESS (7).
```

---

## The 7 Elements

### 1. CHARACTER (The Hero = Your Customer)

**What they want**: A clear, simple desire related to their survival or thriving.

**Questions to Answer**:
- What does your customer want as it relates to your product?
- What desire drives them to seek you out?
- What would their life look like if they got it?

**Good Examples**:
- "Wants to save time on bookkeeping"
- "Wants to feel confident in presentations"
- "Wants a second brain that works for them"

**Bad Examples** (too vague):
- "Wants a better life"
- "Wants success"

### 2. PROBLEM (The Obstacle)

Every story needs a villain - the problem blocking the hero from what they want.

**Three Levels of Problem**:

| Level | Description | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| **External** | Physical, tangible obstacle | "Scattered notes across 10 apps" |
| **Internal** | How it makes them FEEL | "Overwhelmed and frustrated" |
| **Philosophical** | Why this is WRONG | "You shouldn't have to adapt to your tools" |

**The Internal Problem is Most Important**: People buy solutions to internal problems. The external is just the surface.

**Villain Characteristics**:
- Personify the problem
- Make it relatable
- Show what's at stake

### 3. GUIDE (Your Brand)

The guide appears when the hero is stuck. The guide has two characteristics:

**Empathy**: "We understand how frustrating this is"
- Show you've been there
- Acknowledge their struggle
- Use their language

**Authority**: "We've helped thousands solve this"
- Testimonials, credentials
- Results you've achieved
- Proof you can help

**The Balance**: Too much empathy = weak. Too much authority = arrogant. You need both.

### 4. PLAN (The Path to Success)

Heroes need a clear, simple plan. Confusion kills action.

**Two Types of Plans**:

**Process Plan** (3-4 steps max):
```
Step 1: Sign up for free
Step 2: Connect your existing notes
Step 3: Let AI organize everything
Step 4: Never lose an idea again
```

**Agreement Plan** (what they can expect):
```
- We'll never share your data
- Cancel anytime
- Results in 7 days or money back
```

**Rules for Plans**:
- Maximum 3-4 steps
- Simple, concrete actions
- Remove friction and fear
- Show the path is easy

### 5. CALL TO ACTION

Two types of CTAs:

**Direct CTA**: The main action you want them to take
- "Buy Now"
- "Join Waitlist"
- "Start Free Trial"

**Transitional CTA**: Lower commitment, starts relationship
- "Download Free Guide"
- "Watch Demo"
- "Take the Quiz"

**Rules for CTAs**:
- Be clear and specific
- One primary CTA per page
- Repeat the CTA multiple times
- Make it visually obvious

### 6. FAILURE (The Stakes)

What do they lose if they don't act? Stories need stakes.

**Types of Failure**:
- Time wasted
- Money lost
- Opportunities missed
- Continued frustration
- Falling further behind

**How to Use**:
- Paint the picture briefly
- Don't dwell too long (not fear-based marketing)
- Make it real and relatable
- Connect to internal problem

**Example**:
"Without a system, ideas keep slipping through the cracks. That article you meant to save? Gone. That insight from last month? Forgotten. The cost isn't just frustration - it's the compounding loss of your best thinking."

### 7. SUCCESS (The Transformation)

What does life look like after they win?

**Paint the Picture**:
- Specific and visual
- Emotional payoff
- Status transformation
- Before/after contrast

**Example**:
"Imagine opening your laptop and having exactly what you need, exactly when you need it. Every insight you've ever had, organized and searchable. That book you read three years ago? One search away. You're not just organized - you're augmented."

## The BrandScript Template

Use this to document your messaging foundation:

```markdown
# BrandScript: [Product/Brand Name]

## 1. Character
**What they want**: [Clear, simple desire]

## 2. Problem

### External Problem
[The tangible obstacle]

### Internal Problem
[How it makes them feel]

### Philosophical Problem
[Why this is wrong/unjust]

### The Villain
[Personified version of the problem]

## 3. Guide

### Empathy Statement
[Show you understand]

### Authority
[Why you can help]

## 4. Plan

### Step 1: [Action]
[Brief description]

### Step 2: [Action]
[Brief description]

### Step 3: [Action]
[Brief description]

## 5. Call to Action

### Direct CTA
[Main action]

### Transitional CTA
[Lower commitment option]

## 6. Failure
[What they lose if they don't act]

## 7. Success
[What life looks like when they win]

---

## One-Liner
[Character] + [Problem] + [Plan] + [Success]

## Tagline Options
1. [Option 1]
2. [Option 2]
3. [Option 3]
```

## Application to Content

### For Social Posts

**Use Story Elements**:
- **Hook**: State the problem (external or internal)
- **Body**: Introduce the guide (you) with empathy + authority
- **Close**: Call to action or paint success

**Example Thread Structure**:
```
1/ [Problem - External + Internal feeling]
2/ [Why conventional solutions don't work]
3/ [Introduce the guide - empathy]
4/ [The plan - simple steps]
5/ [Success - what life looks like]
6/ [CTA - transitional or direct]
```

### For Landing Pages

**Section Order**:
1. Hero section: Character want + Problem
2. Stakes: What they lose (Failure)
3. Guide: Empathy + Authority
4. Plan: Simple steps
5. Success: Transformation
6. CTA: Direct + Transitional

### For Email

**Subject Line**: Problem or Success promise
**Opening**: Empathy (acknowledge struggle)
**Middle**: Plan (simple solution)
**Close**: CTA + Brief success reminder

## Integration with Other Frameworks

### With Tribal Marketing

| StoryBrand Element | Tribal Application |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| Character | Tribe member |
| Problem | Tribe's shared frustration |
| Guide | Brand with tribe credibility |
| Plan | Path to tribe's desired change |
| CTA | Join the tribe |
| Failure | What happens if tribe loses |
| Success | World the tribe is building |

### With STEPPS

| StoryBrand Element | STEPPS Application |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| Problem | Trigger (common occurrence) |
| Guide | Social Currency (being helped by expert) |
| Plan | Practical Value (useful steps) |
| Success | Emotion (high-arousal positive) |
| Full Story | Stories (narrative arc) |

### With Content Matrix

| Content Type | StoryBrand Focus |
|--------------|-----------------|
| Actionable | Plan (step-by-step) |
| Motivational | Success stories |
| Analytical | Problem (why it exists) |
| Contrarian | Challenge false solutions |
| Comparison | Show superior plan |
| Future-Focused | Success transformation |

## Quality Check

**StoryBrand Checklist for Content**:

- [ ] Is the customer clearly the hero (not us)?
- [ ] Did we acknowledge their problem at all three levels?
- [ ] Do we show empathy before authority?
- [ ] Is the plan simple (3-4 steps max)?
- [ ] Is the CTA clear and specific?
- [ ] Did we paint the failure stakes?
- [ ] Did we show the success transformation?
- [ ] Is the one-liner memorable and clear?

---

## Common Mistakes

1. **Brand as Hero**: Talking about yourself instead of the customer
2. **Unclear Want**: Vague desires instead of specific outcomes
3. **Surface Problems Only**: Missing the internal/philosophical levels
4. **Authority Without Empathy**: Coming across as arrogant
5. **Complex Plans**: More than 4 steps creates confusion
6. **Weak CTA**: Buried or unclear calls to action
7. **No Stakes**: Forgetting to show what they lose
8. **Generic Success**: Vague "better life" instead of specific transformation
